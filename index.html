    National Aerospace Laboratories

    Bangalore, India

    January 2016 - May 2016

    Introduction:

    The present day visual inspection of the aircraft is purely skill based, is time consuming, raises safety issues to the inspector, and there is also lack of record of the inspection. The accuracy can be affected by inspector boredom, weather conditions and weariness. Images captured from the aircraft surface and presented to the inspector after enhancing the defects can make the visual inspection easier and accurate which in turn results in saving time, and ensures the safety of aircraft.

    Problem Statement:

    Development and implementation of algorithms for the detection of surface defect (dents) in aircraft surfaces using image processing. It involves image acquisition, noise removal, background removal, phase wrapping, phase unwrapping and height mapping.

    Objectives:

    The project aims to create a semi-automated system to perform dent detection on the surface of aircraft structures. The objectives include identification of dented regions, classification of the dents from surface features such as writings, markings, textures and patterns and quantification of the detected dent.

    Methodology:

    Use of 2D image processing using edge detection is not good enough for the detection of dents as dents deform the surface into third dimension. Also the use of edge detection will also detect the surface features like writing, markings on the surface also as defects. This problem can be solved by trying to re-create the height information of the surface. Variety of ways are described in literature to carry out the profile mapping using optical profilometry. Fringe pattern based optical profilometry method is adopted in this study using 3-step Phase Shift which involves following steps: 1) digital fringe projection, 2) image acquisition, 3) image processing, 4) fringe analysis, that includes phase detection and phase unwrapping, and 5) height mapping. Digital Fringe Projection involves projecting a fringe pattern onto the object surface. A sinusoidal fringe pattern is used as it has features that counter defocussing and is most apt to be processed by linear filtering. The setup consists of a projection unit, an acquisition unit and a processing or analysis unit. The images of the reference plane as well as of the object are captured with the camera, perpendicular to the surface of the plane or object, and the projector at an angle in the Image Acquisition step. The captured images contain the projected patterns on the reference and object surface. 3 phase shifted images are acquired per surface.
    The images that are captured include the surface that needs to be tested as well as certain unwanted elements, such as the background, shadows and reflected light. Image Processing involves the pre-processing of the captured images to remove the aforementioned unwanted elements. Background removal is performed to improve computation time and avoid redundant processing. Fringe Analysis is the next operation performed on the pre-processed images. Fringe Analysis consists of 2 steps: (i) phase detection, and (ii) phase unwrapping. In phase detection, the image is analysed to produce a wrapped phase map distribution. An inverse tangent function is used to generate phase map from the intensity values of the captured images. The resultant map contains the localised phase values of the pixels. The recovered/estimated phase is obtained in the interval [- π, + π]. In general, true phase may range over an interval greater than 2π. In such cases, phase values contain artificial discontinuities. The process of determining the unknown integral multiple of 2π to be added at each pixel of the wrapped phase map to make it continuous by removing the artificial 2π discontinuities is referred to as phase unwrapping. Phase unwrapping is an important step with many pre-existing algorithms designed to perform the task. The unwrapped phase map obtained contains the true phase values at each pixel location. The phase values are then converted into depth information using Height Mapping. The dent is detected based on the height map, and then quantified to find the relative dent depth and area of the dented region. Calibration is needed to perform accurate image to real world coordinate transformation.
    Unit testing, Integration testing and system testing are done to see if if all the modules are working fine, individually and when they are integrated with other modules. Testing will also help remove bugs from the software. After testing all the modules, GUI was made and the final touches to the software was made.

    We also published a paper based on this research:
    Augustin M.J, Archana Ramesh, Yaswi Jalan, Priyank Singh, Nitin Joy, Amit Gupta, Nitesh Gupta, “Adaptive Background Removal for Fringe Profilometry based Surface Inspection of Aircraft Structures”, National Seminar on Aerospace Structures XIX, February 23-25th, 2017, VIT University, Vellore India

    Indian Institute of Science

    Bangalore, India

    January 2015 - July 2015

    Developed an Android app, and designed its front end, that took diﬀerent Data samples for diﬀerent kinds of signals as inputs and generated graphs indicating the distribution of the sample for each of these signals.

    Functional features:

  • In the transmitter part, there is AM wave generation where the AM wave is generated using input values such as Amplitude, Sampling rate ,frequency and signal type.
  • The other functionality is generation of continuous wave using input parameters such as amplitude, sampling rate, time duration.
    AM equation : Am*sin(2*PI*frequency*t)*(1-cos((2*PI*(frequency/5)*t))+offset,
    and for continuous sine wave
    Am*sin(2*PI*frequency*t) + offset,
    where,
    Am : Amplitude of the carrier,
    frequency: Carrier frequency,
    frequency/5: message frequency is assumed to be one fifth of the carrier frequency,
    offset: is equal to the amplitude. This shifts the entire wave upwards so as to not allow the signal to take negative values.
  • In the receiver part, when the data is loaded, there is FFT graph generation using Tuckis algorithm.

    • Process flow:

  • In the transmitter part,
    a. First the amplitude must be entered, and then the sampling rate. Based on the sampling rate, the frequency range is given based on which the frequency has to be selected. Now if AM signal type is chosen, the AM wave will be generated.
    b. Now with the same parameters, if time duration is entered, a continuous wave is generated.
  • In the receiver part,
    a. By loading data, the graph for the data file is generated.
    Now for the same data ,FFT is generated.
    b. Features for future development of filters have also been kept.

    • Usability Testing for the Bergen Swamp Preservation Society website

    RIT, Rochester, NY

    January 2017 - May 2017

    Objective:

    The goal was to study the Bergen swamp preservation society website to be able to identify the usability issues. The main aim of the study was to make sure that the users could clearly understand and use all the features of the application.

    Purpose:

    We were a team of 6 doing the study, and we collaborated with the developer of the new Bergen Swamp Preservation Society website to go about with our usability studies. The main goal was to see if the user experience in the website mapped to the users conceptual model on how to use the website. The purpose was to detect all the usability issues related to the website and also get feedback from all the participants on the website and suggestions on how to improve the website.

    Stakeholders:

    People from teens to 60+ are our target audience.

    Process:

    The Bergen swamp preservation society website had many functionalities in it like, getting directions, calendar, events, photos and so on.
    The first step in our usability testing study was to do the heuristic evaluations. Initially, all of us, individually used the different heuristics and all the gestalt principles to detect the usability flaws in the website. Post this, we merged all of our findings.
    5 students were recruited as participants via flyers. All potential participants had to take the screener questions, and if they were eligible, they were selected to be participants. A 20$ Amazon gift card was given after the study to all the participants. Pilot tests were done initially with 2 participants, Test design was made and a test plan was formulated.
    5 tasks were created and all of them were based on problem scenarios. These tasks were shuffled between all participants so that there is no transfer of knowledge between tasks and counterbalance the tasks.
    The success criteria for each task was predetermined and decided upon by all the team members in advance. Qualitative and quantitative analysis of the data was done. Qualitative analysis was done by asking the participants questions after every task, quantitative analysis was done by counting the number of clicks and the time taken for every task.
    SUS scale was used to measure the usability of the system

    Setup:

    The usability test was done in the usability lab at B. Thomas Golisano College of Computing and Information Sciences. There were two rooms, a testing room and an observation room. There was a one was mirror between them, where only the observers could see the people in the testing room. The morae software (Morae-observer and Morae-observer) was used to record all the observations of the observers. There was a web camera and microphone to record and listen to the participants. Based on the input, feedback and suggestions made by the participants, a report was written. We presented the whole usability study to the client and submitted the report with the recommendations and usability issues present in the website.

    Accoutre

    RIT, Rochester, NY

    January 2017 - May 2017

    Accoutre is a mobile application for dressing, that is aimed at providing suggestions and allowing its users to pick out clothes, either (i) based on the current weather outside, or the weather throughout the day, or/and (ii) based on an occasion or an event that they are to attend, such as a party, wedding, interview, sports activity, and so on, scheduled on their calendar or in their emails. The designing of the app was done using adobe photoshop. Primary stakeholders of our application would be the end users who own and use a smartphone, and ones who need help in picking out clothes based on the current weather outside, or an event they are to attend.

    Contextual Inquiry:

    We conducted contextual inquiries for 12 participants in total. The interviews were conducted via video calls, or in person. We interviewed both male and female participants, between the ages of 20 to 60+ years, and who owned a smartphone. To ensure that the data we collected from our contextual inquiries came from a relatively diverse set of end users, each of our group members interviewed participants who were located at different geographical regions, with varying climates and weather conditions. The interviews also provided us with different scenarios for the type of occasions or events that the participants were most likely to attend or dress for. The interviews also provided us with different scenarios for the type of occasions or events that the participants were to attend or dress for.
    We gathered important information about the factors influencing the daily routine of picking out clothes for our participants. Most users used a weather application on their smartphones to check the weather, and then proceeded with selecting their clothes. While a few users, mostly college students, picked out their clothes randomly. We also came across a user who took suggestions from their friends and family, to select their outfit for a special occasion or event. The interviews also provided us with different scenarios for the type of occasions or events that the participants were most likely to attend or dress for.

    Design Ideation and Brainstorming:

    From the data collected through our contextual inquiries, we could identify the primary stakeholders of our application, that is, the end users who own and use a smartphone, and ones who need help in picking out clothes based on the current weather outside, or an event they are to attend. Based on the information gathered from our study models – flow models, sequence models, artifact models, physical models, and cultural models, we consolidated our findings into an affinity diagram, and a consolidated sequence model. In general, our key observations from ‘walking the wall’, with respect to the primary workflows for users to pick out their clothes were as follows:
  • Users check the current weather outside or the weather throughout the day, using a reliable weather app, before picking out clothes for that day.
  • They select clothes based on an occasion or an event, such as a party, wedding, interview, sports activity, and so on.
  • They pick out clothes randomly.
  • They use applications such as Instagram or Pinterest to follow fashion trends and be aware.

    • Specific Tasks and App Features:

    To narrow down on the design ideas for our application from the users' perspective, we identified the following tasks and features as they relate to the goals:
  • Sync with the calendar application.
  • Access to user’s current location.
  • Access and connect to the weather application.
  • Sort clothes based on categories, for example – type, fabric or season.
  • Option to feed-in clothes present in user’s current wardrobe under each category.
  • Run algorithms to choose and suggest clothes based on weather, events, or fashion trends.
  • Option to choose and mix-and-match clothes manually, in case the algorithm gets it wrong.
  • Suggestion to shop for clothes online, based on current requirement and unavailability in user’s closet.
  • Suggestion of suitable online retailers, and direction to their official websites.
    • Based on the information gathered, we have generated the various study models, and have consolidated our findings into an affinity diagram, and a consolidated sequence model. We were successful in gathering sufficient information to help us understand users’ patterns of picking out clothes, which would help us tremendously in the next phase of our project.

    Future Improvements:

    For future work, the aspects of relevant tasks and activities that could be improved are as follows:
  • The application would help users to conjure a virtual image of how they would look in an outfit.
  • It would allow users to connect with their friends/family, share images of outfits with them, and take suggestions.
  • It would notify users about online offers or sales by their favorite retailers, or based on their current wardrobe requirement.
  • It would allow users to save and maintain their online shopping history.

    • Problem Scenario:

    Each morning, Jenny wakes up and checks the calendar application and e-mail notifications on her phone. She then takes a shower, and randomly picks out clothes to wear to work. Since her office is 10 minutes away from her home, she usually walks to work. One day, on her way to work, it started to pour suddenly. Because Jenny was not prepared for the rain, and did not have a raincoat or an umbrella with her, her clothes and bag got completely drenched.

    Design Scenario:

    Ever since Jenny started using the Accoutre app, she wakes up each morning and checks the app, along with other notifications on her phone. She then takes a shower, and wears the clothes suggested by the Accoutre app, based on the current weather outside, and/or an occasion or event she must attend, like a boarding meeting or a party. The app helps her pick out the appropriate outfit each time.

    Coaster Crunch

    RIT, Rochester, NY

    September 2017 - Present

    Introduction:

    The most common problem that big amusement parks face today is huge crowds which lead to long wait times and long lines for each ride in the park. The wait times can be so long that it can make the people get so frustrated and not wanting to come back again. This app is designed to bring in a solution to this problem faced by bigger amusement parks.

    Problem:

    We all want to spend our leisure time with family and friends especially when we're loaded with work. A theme park is often one of the most favourite places for people to relax, have fun on the rides and forget their troubles for a brief period of time. But isn’t it exhausting to wait in a seemingly never ending queue to get on every single ride? During peak seasons there are queues which could go on up to an hour or even more for every ride. This could be a frustrating problem to a lot of people who have spent a lot of money on the tickets and don’t get to go on all or most of the rides in the park. The whole experience of waiting in a queue for an hour or more for a ride that lasts a few minutes can be stressful, exhausting and demoralizing at times.

    Solution:

    A solution for this would be to introduce flash passes or in other words to introduce points for every ride. For instance, let’s say we have one point per ride, and the people have to scan they phone with the barcode for every ride. After two rides, the person can get a fastpass for the next ride, that he has queued up, for which there would be there would be a separate queue where only the people who have accumulated the points and who have that ride queued up for themselves will be able to enter. In this way people would be able to complete most of the rides in an amusement park. The concept of a flash pass has already been initiated by Disney for their amusement parks, however an application does not exist which makes it very exhausting for the people to walk up to each ride, get a pass for that ride and come back at the time that is mentioned on the pass. However, our objective is to digitize the entire concept so we can develop an app. The application will keep track of all the points accumulated by the park visitors which can be used at any time during the year. The app will also give notification of options on which rides can be accessed without facing the queues. This solution falls under ‘Market or population characterization for new system concepts’ which will require us to understand the needs of our target users and develop a new in-house system for existing theme parks.

    Customer:

    More businesses in this day and age are reverting to using user data as part of their process strategy. In this particular case, the target user is quite clear – people who visit theme parks multiple times a year. Gaining user data is core towards the successful implementation of our solution.

    Stakeholders:

    There are two key stakeholders that we need to focus on to ensure practical application of the proposed solution – the people who frequent theme parks annually and the businesses who own them. The goal of this process is to ensure that customers who visit theme parks multiple times in a year are given the opportunity to enjoy future visits without the added pressure of waiting in long lines which would encourage them to increase their visits. The businesses will be able to reward their most loyal customers and attract new ones who would then turn into loyal customers which would drastically increase the brand value of company which owns the theme park.

    Design Process:

    The design process included conducting contextual inquiries. We needed to conduct contextual inquiries in order to get the views of all our groups of stakeholders. We designed a list of questions and a screener questionnaire. We then recruited participants. Some of our groups of participants were children, moms, people managing the park and teens. Once we were done with the interviews, we had to analyze and consolidate our data. To do this, we had a brainstorming session where we made our affinity diagram as shown below from the data we had collected from the participants.

    Once this was done, we made user profile personas for all the user profiles present, w.r.t. our scenario. The personas are as shown below. We made both the primary and the secondary personas and also came up with an anti-persona. These are as shown below.
    Now once we had the personas done, the next step for our team was to build the low-fi prototype. For this, we looked at our affinity diagram and personas to gather in all our data from it. From the data collected, we designed our screen’s content first, post which we drew the low-fi prototypes. The app will include functionalities such as buying daily and seasonal passes, list of rides and with barcodes to scan and storing the rides finished, issuing of fast passes and also designing virtual reality games for people while they are standing in line.
